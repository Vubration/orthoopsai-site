Why Most Review Requests Fail

Here's the typical review request small business owners send:

"Hi [Name], thank you for your recent visit! We'd love it if you could take a moment to leave us a Google review. Your feedback means a lot to us. [link]"

It reads like a mass email. It feels like one too — because it usually is. The customer clicks the link, sees a blank review box, has no idea what to write, and closes the tab. Review not written.

What actually works:

  • Timing: Within 24 hours of the positive experience, not 3 days later
  • Personalization: Use their name. Reference something specific.
  • Direction: Tell them what to write about. Remove the blank-page paralysis.
  • Length: Under 60 words. Long messages don't get read.
  • Channel: Text > email for local service businesses. 98% open rate vs. 20%.
💡 The real blocker isn't your customers' willingness — it's their uncertainty. When your message says "mention how fast we responded and whether you'd recommend us," they have something to write. The review takes 90 seconds instead of feeling like homework.

The AI Prompt That Generates It

Here's the prompt from the Small Business Owner AI Toolkit that writes this message for you every time:

Write a short, casual message (email or text) to send to a recent customer named [NAME] who had a great experience at [BUSINESS NAME]. Ask them to leave a Google review. Include a direct link placeholder [GOOGLE REVIEW LINK], a specific prompt for what to write about (their experience, what they loved), and keep the entire message under 60 words. Sound like a real person, not a corporation running a review campaign.

Paste that into ChatGPT or Claude, fill in the brackets, and you'll get something like this:

"Hey Sarah — really glad the install went smoothly today! If you get a sec, would you mind leaving us a quick Google review? Even just mentioning how the crew did and whether you'd call us again would be huge. Here's the link: [GOOGLE REVIEW LINK]. Takes about 60 seconds. Thanks so much!"

That message gets read. And it gets responses — because it sounds like a person sent it.

Variations: Email vs. Text vs. In-Person

Different businesses need different delivery formats. Here are three prompt variations depending on how you typically communicate with customers:

Text message (service businesses, HVAC, plumbers, salons):

Write a casual text message (under 60 words) to send to a recent service customer named [NAME] within a few hours of their appointment at [BUSINESS NAME]. Ask them to leave a Google review at [LINK]. Tell them specifically to mention [what service they had and one thing we want highlighted]. Sound like the technician or owner personally following up, not a marketing system.

Email (retail, restaurants, gyms):

Write a short follow-up email (under 100 words) to [NAME] who visited [BUSINESS NAME] on [DATE]. Subject line should feel personal, not promotional. Body: thank them briefly, ask for a Google review at [LINK], and suggest they mention [specific aspect — e.g., "the wait time, the staff, whether the food matched the photos"]. One clear call to action. No corporate language.

In-person ask (to be said by staff):

Write a natural, conversational script (3-4 sentences) for a [BUSINESS TYPE] employee to say to a customer at checkout when asking for a Google review. The customer just had [DESCRIBE THE POSITIVE INTERACTION]. Staff should reference what went well, hand the customer a card with the QR code or URL, and make the ask feel genuine rather than scripted. Don't say "if you get a chance" — make the ask direct but friendly.
💡 Pro tip: Print a QR code linking to your Google review page and hand it to customers at checkout. Combine it with the in-person script above. The QR code removes the "I'll do it later" delay — they can tap and leave the review before they leave your parking lot.

What to Do After You Get the Review

Responding to every review — good and bad — is as important as getting them. Google uses response rate as a signal. Customers read your responses to negative reviews to judge how you handle problems.

Here's the prompt for responding to a 5-star review:

Write a warm, genuine response to a 5-star Google review from [CUSTOMER NAME] who said: "[PASTE REVIEW TEXT]". We are [BUSINESS NAME], a [TYPE OF BUSINESS] in [CITY]. Thank them by name, reference something specific they mentioned, and invite them back. Keep it under 80 words. Do not sound like a corporate template.

And for a negative review:

Write a professional, empathetic response to this negative Google review from [CUSTOMER NAME]: "[PASTE REVIEW]". We are [BUSINESS NAME], a [BUSINESS TYPE] in [CITY]. Acknowledge their experience without admitting fault, express genuine concern, mention a specific step we've taken to address this type of issue, and invite them to contact us directly at [PHONE/EMAIL]. Keep it under 120 words. Do not be defensive or argue with their claims.
💡 Responding to a negative review publicly is one of the highest-leverage things you can do for your reputation. Future customers read your responses and judge your character. Empathetic, solution-oriented responses consistently outperform silence — even when the reviewer is wrong.
