ChatGPT Prompts for E-commerce & Shopify Store Owners: Copy-Paste Templates That Sell

Published April 19, 2026 · 9 min read · Filed under: E-commerce, AI Prompts, Shopify, Marketing

Running an online store means writing constantly. Product descriptions. Abandoned cart emails. Customer service replies. Influencer outreach DMs. Sale announcements. Win-back campaigns. If you're manually writing each one from scratch — or using the same stale copy your store launched with three years ago — you're burning time that could go into sourcing, scaling, or sleeping.

The stores growing fastest aren't hiring bigger copy teams. They're using AI to handle the writing pipeline so their humans can focus on strategy. The problem isn't using ChatGPT — it's knowing how to prompt it so the output is actually usable and not painfully generic.

This guide gives you five real, copy-paste ChatGPT prompts for the writing situations e-commerce sellers face most. Drop them into ChatGPT, fill in the brackets, and publish.

Why Most E-commerce Sellers Waste Time with AI

Ask ChatGPT to "write a product description for my candle" and you'll get something that could apply to any candle on any store on the internet. It's technically a product description. It's also completely useless for conversion.

The difference between output you delete and copy you actually publish is in the specificity of the prompt. You need to give the AI the context that makes your product your product — the scent notes, the burn time, the customer your store is for, the tone of your brand, the one thing that separates you from the Walmart version. These templates handle that structure for you. You fill in the specific variables. The AI does the writing.

Template 1: Product Description That Converts

Use this when: You're launching a new product, refreshing old listings, or trying to improve conversion rate on underperforming pages. Most product descriptions lead with features. Winning product descriptions lead with the feeling — then back it up with facts. This prompt generates both.

You are a conversion-focused copywriter for an e-commerce store. Write a product description that leads with emotion and backs it up with specifics. My store type / niche: [e.g., premium home goods / outdoor gear / natural skincare / handmade jewelry] Product name: [e.g., Cedar + Smoke Soy Candle — 8 oz] Key features: - [e.g., 45-hour burn time] - [e.g., hand-poured, small batch] - [e.g., lead-free cotton wick] - [e.g., scent notes: cedar, black pepper, light smoke] Who buys this: [e.g., 28-45 year old women who buy gifts for themselves — quality-conscious, hate synthetic fragrance] The one thing that separates this from cheaper alternatives: [e.g., we source fragrance from the same supplier as $60 candles — and sell for $28] Price point: [e.g., $28] Tone: [e.g., warm and elevated / playful / minimal and direct] Write 3 versions: 1. Benefit-forward (opens with what the customer feels, not what the product is) 2. Story-driven (opens with a moment or scene) 3. SEO-optimized (opens with the primary keyword, includes 4-5 keyword variants naturally) Each version: 80-120 words. No clichés ("elevate your," "perfect for," "indulge yourself").
💡 Pro move: Run all three versions on a new product and A/B test them. Most Shopify stores never test their product copy — even a 5% lift in conversion rate compounds massively at scale. Use the winning version as your template for the whole product line.

Template 2: Abandoned Cart Email Sequence (3-Touch)

Use this when: A customer added to cart but didn't check out. Industry average cart abandonment is 70%+. A well-timed 3-email sequence recovers a meaningful chunk of that revenue with zero ad spend. Most stores send one email. The third email in a good sequence typically outperforms the first by 2-3x because you've already filtered to the most interested abandoners.

You are an email copywriter for an e-commerce store. Write a 3-email abandoned cart sequence that recovers sales without being annoying. Store name: [e.g., Clementine & Co.] Store type / vibe: [e.g., handmade jewelry, warm and personal / sustainable outdoor gear, direct and minimal] Product abandoned: [e.g., Sterling Silver Initial Necklace — $68] Key selling point: [e.g., made-to-order, ships in 3-5 days / low stock — only 4 left] Discount policy: [e.g., offer 10% in Email 3 only / no discount — compete on value] Customer first name: [use "{{first_name}}" as the merge tag placeholder] Email 1 — send 1 hour after abandonment: Subject line options: 3 options, no "You left something behind" (cliché) Body: Under 80 words. Friendly, not pushy. One CTA button. Email 2 — send 24 hours after abandonment: Subject line options: 3 options, social proof or urgency angle Body: Under 100 words. Include one line of social proof (e.g., "Over 2,400 customers have ordered this piece"). One CTA. Email 3 — send 72 hours after abandonment: Subject line options: 3 options — final offer angle Body: Under 100 words. If offering discount, introduce it here only. Create light urgency. Final CTA. Tone: Match store vibe. No high-pressure tactics. Conversational.
💡 Pro move: Set up this sequence once in Klaviyo or Shopify Email with the 1h / 24h / 72h delays and it runs forever. Recover 5-15% of abandoned carts automatically. This is one of the highest-ROI automations any e-commerce store can run — and AI writes the copy in 2 minutes.

Template 3: Win-Back Campaign for Lapsed Customers

Use this when: A customer who's purchased before has gone quiet — typically 90+ days since their last order. Win-back emails work because you're not selling to a cold lead — you're re-activating someone who already trusted you with their credit card. The pitch is "we miss you," not "buy something."

You are an email copywriter. Write a 2-email win-back sequence for lapsed e-commerce customers. Store name: [e.g., Birchwood Supply Co.] What the store sells: [e.g., premium woodworking tools and supplies for hobbyist woodworkers] Lapsed threshold: [e.g., 90 days since last order / 6 months] Customer segment: [e.g., customers who ordered twice but haven't been back / one-time buyers only] Win-back offer (if any): [e.g., 15% off their next order / free shipping on next order / no discount — just reconnect] Customer first name merge tag: [e.g., {{first_name}}] Email 1 — "We miss you" (no offer): Subject options: 3 (warm, curious, not needy) Body: Under 100 words. Reference what they bought or the category. Ask a simple question (e.g., "Are you still working on that project?"). CTA is low-pressure — browse, not buy. Email 2 — Final offer (7 days after Email 1, only to non-openers or non-clickers): Subject options: 3 (final touch, can be slightly more direct) Body: Under 100 words. Introduce the offer. Light urgency (expires in 5 days). Clear CTA. Tone: Like a brand they actually like is checking in — not a corporate re-engagement blast.

Template 4: Influencer & UGC Creator Outreach DM

Use this when: You're reaching out to micro-influencers or UGC creators for product gifting or paid collaboration. The DM that fails is long, formal, and reads like a press release. The DM that works is short, specific, and makes the creator feel like you actually know their content — not that you copy-pasted the same message to 200 accounts.

You are a brand partnership manager. Write 3 versions of a DM to send to a social media creator for a product gifting or paid collaboration. My brand: [e.g., Clementine & Co. — handmade sterling silver jewelry] Product I want to gift/feature: [e.g., our bestselling Initial Necklace — $68 retail] Creator niche: [e.g., lifestyle / fashion / home decor / fitness] Creator platform: [e.g., Instagram / TikTok] Collaboration type: [e.g., gifting only — post if they love it / paid UGC ($75 flat fee, no post required) / affiliate (15% commission)] Creator following size: [e.g., 8,000 followers / 50,000 followers] One specific detail about their content I can mention: [e.g., I loved your recent NYC apartment tour / your product review format is really authentic] Version 1: Gifting pitch — short (under 60 words), casual, feels hand-typed Version 2: Paid UGC pitch — slightly more detailed, explains deliverable clearly Version 3: Affiliate pitch — leads with earning potential, minimal friction to start All three: No "I'd love to partner with you" (overused). No emojis overload. Sound like a real person, not a brand intern following a template.
💡 Pro move: Micro-influencers (5K-50K) typically convert better than mega-influencers for niche e-commerce products. Their audiences trust them more. Use the gifting DM template for nano accounts, the UGC version for mid-tier, and the affiliate version for anyone with a proven track record of driving sales.

Template 5: Seasonal Sale Email (Black Friday / Holiday / Flash Sale)

Use this when: You're running a sale and need email copy that stands out in an inbox that's getting 40 promotional emails that same day. Most sale emails lead with "SALE — 30% OFF EVERYTHING." They all look the same. The ones that cut through lead with a story, a reason, or a hook — then hit the offer.

You are a promotional email copywriter. Write a high-converting sale announcement email for an e-commerce store. Store name: [e.g., Rootbound Plant Co.] What you sell: [e.g., rare houseplants and ceramic planters] Sale type: [e.g., Black Friday / holiday gift guide / summer clearance / flash sale (24 hours)] Discount: [e.g., 25% off sitewide / buy 2 get 1 free / free shipping over $40] Sale dates: [e.g., November 29-December 1 / today only — ends midnight EST] Best sellers to feature: [e.g., Monstera Albo cuttings, Terracotta planter set, hanging plant shelf] Audience: [e.g., plant lovers aged 25-40, city dwellers with small spaces] Tone: [e.g., playful and punny / warm and genuine / minimal and bold] Write: 1. Subject line — 3 options (one curiosity, one urgency, one benefit-led) 2. Preview text — 3 options (25-40 characters each) 3. Email body — 3 versions: a. Story-open version (30-50 word story hook, then offer) b. Direct version (offer above the fold, minimal copy, one CTA) c. Product-hero version (highlight 3 specific products with 1-sentence descriptions, then offer) Each body version: under 150 words. One CTA button. No "Don't miss out!" (cliché).
💡 Pro move: Segment your list before sending. Your most loyal buyers (2+ orders, opened last 3 emails) get Version A — they're warm enough for a story. Your lapsed segment gets Version B — just get to the point. Your newer subscribers get Version C — show them the product first. Same sale, three audiences, meaningfully higher revenue.
