Every one of these is something AI can do in under 60 seconds — if you have the right prompt. Here's how.

Section 1: Estimate Follow-Up Prompts (Stop Losing Jobs to Silence)

Most home service businesses send a quote and then... wait. Maybe one follow-up call, maybe none. The reality is that most customers need 2–3 touches before they commit, especially for larger jobs. Here are prompts to handle that automatically.

Prompt 1 — Day-3 Follow-Up Text
Write a short, friendly text message I can send to a customer 3 days after I gave them a quote for [TYPE OF WORK, e.g., full AC replacement]. The estimate was $[AMOUNT]. They haven't responded yet. Keep it casual, not pushy — I want to check in, answer any questions, and remind them the quote is valid for 30 days. My business name is [BUSINESS NAME]. Under 100 words.

This one alone recovers jobs. Most competitors don't follow up at all. A friendly nudge at day 3, day 7, and day 14 beats a high-pressure call every time.

Prompt 2 — Price Objection Script
A customer just texted me saying "I got a cheaper quote from someone else." Write a professional, confident reply that: 1. Acknowledges their concern without being defensive 2. Briefly explains what's included in my quote (warranty, licensed tech, cleanup, follow-up) 3. Offers to hop on a 5-minute call to walk them through the difference 4. Doesn't bad-mouth the competitor My business: [NAME]. Job type: [HVAC install / roof repair / electrical panel upgrade / etc.]
Prompt 3 — Quote Urgency Add-On
I sent a customer a quote for [JOB TYPE] two weeks ago. They still haven't decided. Write a follow-up message I can send that creates a little urgency without being manipulative. I can offer a $[AMOUNT] discount if they book by Friday because my schedule opens up. Keep it honest and friendly. Under 80 words.

Section 2: Google Review Response Prompts

Every response to a Google review is indexed. It's not just customer service — it's SEO. Here's how to use AI to handle reviews fast and well.

Prompt 4 — Respond to a 5-Star Review
Write a warm, genuine response to this Google review for my [HVAC / plumbing / roofing / etc.] business: "[PASTE THE REVIEW]" Include my business name: [NAME]. Keep it 3-4 sentences, personal (not corporate), and mention the type of work if they did. End with an invitation to call us anytime.
Prompt 5 — Respond to a Negative Review (Without Burning Down)
Write a professional, calm response to this negative Google review: "[PASTE THE REVIEW]" My business: [NAME]. My side of the story: [BRIEF EXPLANATION — e.g., "Customer was upset about scheduling delay caused by a parts shortage we communicated upfront."] The response should: - Acknowledge their frustration without admitting wrongdoing - Show I take it seriously - Offer to resolve it directly (not publicly) - Not be longer than 100 words

Pro tip: Never copy-paste without editing. Add one specific detail that proves you remember the job (first name, neighborhood, date). It signals to other readers that you're a real person, not a bot.

Section 3: Seasonal Maintenance Campaigns

Your customer list is gold. Most home service businesses collect names and numbers and then... do nothing with them. Here's how to run a simple seasonal outreach campaign in 10 minutes with AI.

Prompt 6 — Spring HVAC Tune-Up Text Blast
Write 3 short text message variations I can send to past customers promoting a spring AC tune-up special. My business is [NAME], I serve [CITY/AREA], and the tune-up is $[PRICE]. Each version should be under 80 words. Make Version A friendly/casual, Version B urgency-focused (limited slots), and Version C deal-focused.
Prompt 7 — Winter Plumbing Freeze Prevention Reminder
Write an email I can send to past plumbing customers as a pre-winter reminder about pipe freeze prevention. Include 3 practical DIY tips they can do themselves (build trust, don't be salesy) and a soft mention that if they want a professional inspection before the cold hits, we have openings in November. Business: [NAME]. Keep it under 300 words.

These two prompts alone can generate real revenue in the slow season. If you have 200 past customers and even 5% book — that's 10 jobs you didn't have to hustle for.

Section 4: Job Posting & Hiring

Trades hiring is brutal right now. Here's how to post jobs that actually attract quality applicants instead of getting ghosted by everyone who applies.

Prompt 8 — Job Ad for a Skilled Technician
Write a job listing for an [HVAC technician / licensed electrician / plumber / roofer] position at my company, [NAME], based in [CITY]. Key details: - Pay range: $[MIN]–$[MAX]/hr depending on experience - Benefits: [LIST WHAT YOU OFFER — e.g., paid vacation, company vehicle, health insurance, overtime] - We're looking for someone with [X] years experience and [any certifications] Make the tone feel like a good place to work, not a corporate job listing. End with a clear call to action (text or email to apply). Under 250 words.
Prompt 9 — Interview Question Set for a Trades Hire
Write 10 interview questions for a [HVAC tech / plumber / electrician / roofer] position that will help me figure out: 1. Their actual skill level (not just what's on their resume) 2. How they handle customer interactions on the job 3. How they deal with unexpected problems mid-job 4. Whether they'll show up reliably Include 3 situational questions ("Tell me about a time...") and 2 technical questions relevant to the trade.

Section 5: Scope Creep & Upsell Conversations

One of the hardest parts of running a trades business is having the conversation when a job gets bigger than the original quote — or when you notice something else that needs attention while you're already there. AI can help you find the right words.

Prompt 10 — Scope Change Conversation Script
Write a short, professional script I can use when I'm on a job and I discover additional work that wasn't in the original estimate. The situation: [DESCRIBE — e.g., "I'm replacing a water heater and I can see the supply lines are badly corroded. It'll add $150 to the job."] The script should: - Clearly explain what I found - Explain why it matters (risk if left alone) - State the cost honestly - Not feel like an upsell — feel like I'm looking out for them
Prompt 11 — Maintenance Agreement Pitch Script
Write a friendly pitch I can use when finishing a job to offer a maintenance agreement or service plan. My business is [NAME]. The plan is $[PRICE/year] and includes [LIST WHAT'S INCLUDED — e.g., annual tune-up, priority scheduling, 10% discount on parts]. Make it feel like I'm doing them a favor by offering it, not pushing a sale. Under 150 words. Should work as something I say out loud at the door, not a formal proposal.
