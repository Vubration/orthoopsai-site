ChatGPT Templates for Contractors: Copy-Paste Prompts That Win More Jobs

Published April 18, 2026 · 8 min read · Filed under: Contractor Tools, AI Prompts

You're great at the work. The writing? That's where most contractors bleed time and money.

Think about everything you have to write in a single week: a proposal for a roofing job, a follow-up email when a customer goes quiet after getting your estimate, a polite-but-firm response when someone says "your competitor quoted me less," a text asking a happy customer to leave you a Google review. None of it is complicated — but it all takes time, and most contractors either do it poorly, do it inconsistently, or just don't do it at all.

ChatGPT changes this completely — but only if you give it the right prompts. Generic instructions get generic results. Specific, field-tested prompts built for how contractors actually work get results you can actually use.

This guide gives you five real, copy-paste ChatGPT templates for contractors across the most common situations you face. Use them as-is or drop them into a full toolkit.

Why Generic ChatGPT Prompts Fail Contractors

Most people open ChatGPT and type something like "write me a proposal for a roofing job." What they get back is a generic, corporate-sounding document that doesn't reflect how they actually talk to customers, doesn't have their company name or pricing structure baked in, and takes another 20 minutes of back-and-forth to get usable.

The secret to getting great output from any AI is specificity. The more context you give it — your trade, your situation, the customer's objection, the exact outcome you want — the better the result. That's what good ChatGPT templates do. They front-load the context so the AI produces something you can actually send.

Here are five templates that do exactly that.

Template 1: The Winning Project Proposal

Use this when: You need a professional written proposal that clearly explains your scope, timeline, and price — and positions you above the competition.

You are a professional contractor writing a project proposal for a potential client. Write a clear, professional project proposal for the following job: Trade/service: [HVAC / roofing / plumbing / electrical — fill in yours] Job description: [describe the specific project in 1-2 sentences] Estimated timeline: [X days / weeks] Price: $[amount] Warranty or guarantee: [describe what you offer] My company name: [Your Company] My contact info: [phone / email] The proposal should: - Open with a professional summary of the project and what you'll deliver - Include a clear scope of work section with 4-6 bullet points of exactly what's included (and optionally what's excluded) - Mention the timeline and payment terms - Close with a call to action ("Sign and return" or "Reply to accept") - Sound professional but not stiff — like a real contractor who knows what they're doing Keep it under 400 words. Format it so I can copy-paste into an email or PDF.

Once you fill in the brackets, ChatGPT will hand you a polished proposal in seconds. Adjust the tone slider ("more formal" or "more conversational") if needed — most customers respond better to something that sounds human.

Template 2: The Estimate Follow-Up Sequence

Use this when: A customer got your estimate and then went quiet. Most contractors give up after one follow-up. This prompt generates a three-touch sequence that stays professional without being annoying.

Write a 3-email follow-up sequence for a contractor who gave a customer an estimate and hasn't heard back. The emails should feel human, not pushy or desperate. Trade: [fill in your trade] Estimate amount: $[amount] Job type: [describe the project] Customer name: [First name] My company name: [Your Company] Email 1 (send 3 days after estimate): Brief, friendly check-in. Ask if they have any questions about the scope or price. One sentence max. Email 2 (send 7 days after estimate): Slightly warmer. Mention that your schedule has openings and you want to give them priority. Still no pressure. Email 3 (send 14 days after estimate): Final attempt. Let them know you'll close the estimate out but you're happy to revisit if timing changes. Leave the door open. Each email should be 3-5 sentences max. Subject lines included. Professional tone throughout.
💡 Pro tip: Paste the three emails into a saved template in your email client (Gmail Canned Responses, Outlook Quick Parts). The next time you need a follow-up, it's two clicks.

Template 3: The Google Review Request

Use this when: A job went great and you want to ask for a review without it being awkward. Most contractors either never ask, or they send something so generic that customers ignore it. This prompt gets you a natural, personal ask.

Write a short, friendly text message (under 160 characters) asking a happy customer to leave a Google review. The message should: - Use their first name - Reference the specific job (so it feels personal, not mass-blasted) - Include a direct link placeholder: [YOUR GOOGLE REVIEW LINK] - Sound like it was written by a person, not a marketing department - Not be pushy or desperate Customer name: [First name] Job completed: [describe the job in 3-5 words] My name: [Your first name] Also write a follow-up version to send 5 days later if they didn't respond to the first one. Same rules — friendly, brief, personal.

Google reviews are one of the highest-ROI activities for a trades business. A 10-review lead over a competitor can be the difference between getting the call and not getting the call. Two text messages — one right after the job and one follow-up — are enough to dramatically increase your review volume.

Template 4: Handling the Price Shopper

Use this when: A customer says "your competitor quoted me less" or "can you match this price?" This is one of the highest-stakes conversations in contracting — handled wrong, you either lose the job or give away margin for nothing. This template helps you respond professionally, hold your value, and often win the job anyway.

Write a professional, confident email response for a contractor dealing with a customer who says their price is higher than a competitor's quote. My trade: [fill in your trade] My price: $[amount] What the competitor apparently quoted: $[amount or "lower — I don't know the exact number"] Key differentiators I offer: [e.g., licensed and insured, 5-year warranty, family-owned 12 years, no subcontractors] The response should: - Acknowledge the customer's concern without being defensive - Explain the value difference without attacking the competitor - Reinforce 2-3 specific reasons my price is justified - Offer to do a quick call to walk through the scope in detail - Close with confidence — I'm not desperate, but I do want the job Tone: Professional, warm, and confident. Not salesy. Under 250 words.

Most customers who say "the other guy is cheaper" are actually looking for a reason to justify hiring you. They just need you to give them one. This prompt helps you do that without sounding defensive or dropping your price unnecessarily.

Template 5: Seasonal Maintenance Campaign Email

Use this when: It's the start of a season and you want to proactively reach past customers for tune-ups, inspections, or maintenance work. This is one of the easiest ways to generate revenue from your existing customer base — most contractors leave it on the table entirely.

Write a seasonal outreach email to past customers for a [HVAC / plumbing / roofing / electrical — fill in your trade] contractor. Season: [spring / summer / fall / winter] Service being promoted: [e.g., AC tune-up, furnace inspection, roof inspection after storm season, winterization] Special offer (optional): [e.g., 10% off, free filter change with tune-up, priority scheduling] My company name: [Your Company] My contact info: [phone or link to book] The email should: - Open with a seasonal hook (what's coming up that makes this relevant NOW) - Explain the specific service and why it matters this time of year - Include the offer if applicable - End with a clear call to action ("Reply to book" or "Call us at...") - Feel like it's from a real local business, not a corporate template Length: 150-200 words. Subject line included.
