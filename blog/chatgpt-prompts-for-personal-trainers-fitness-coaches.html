Why Most Trainers Get Bad Results From ChatGPT

The problem isn't ChatGPT — it's the prompt. Type "write me a client check-in message" and you'll get something generic, flat, and suspiciously corporate. Nobody who sweats with people for a living actually communicates like that.

Good prompts give ChatGPT the context it needs: the client's situation, your coaching style, the specific outcome you're going for, and any constraints (tone, length, compliance). The difference between a mediocre output and a message you'd actually send is almost entirely about how much detail you put in up front. These templates do that work for you.

Template 1: The Weekly Client Check-In Message

Use this when: You need to send a mid-week or end-of-week check-in to a client — one that feels personal, not automated. Multiply this across 20–30 clients and it's a significant time sink every week. This prompt makes it fast without making it feel copy-pasted.

You are a personal trainer writing a weekly check-in message to a client. Write a warm, brief check-in message (under 100 words) that feels personal and motivating. Client name: [First name] Current goal: [e.g., fat loss, muscle gain, marathon training, post-injury rehab] Last session focus: [e.g., lower body strength, ran 5 miles, upper body push] One thing they struggled with or mentioned last time: [e.g., tight hips, diet slipping on weekends, sleep issues] My coaching style: [e.g., direct and results-focused / warm and encouraging / tough love / laid-back and supportive] The message should: - Reference something specific from our last session so it feels personal - Ask one simple question to prompt a response (accountability) - End on an encouraging note - Sound like a real human text, not an email newsletter No bullet points. Casual but professional tone.
💡 Time-saver move: Keep a running note with each client's name, goal, and last session focus. Before check-in day, update the "last session" field for each and batch-generate all your messages in one ChatGPT session. What used to take 45 minutes takes 8.

Template 2: The New Client Onboarding Sequence

Use this when: A new client just signed up and you want to set the tone, gather information, and start building the relationship before session one. Most trainers either skip this entirely or send a boring intake form. This sequence does both — it's professional and personal at the same time.

Write a 3-message onboarding sequence for a new personal training client. The messages should feel warm and professional — like a real coach who cares about results, not a gym franchise. My name: [Your name] My coaching focus: [e.g., strength training for beginners / weight loss for busy moms / athletic performance / online coaching for remote clients] Client name: [First name] Their goal: [what they shared when signing up] Training format: [in-person / online / hybrid] Message 1 (send immediately after sign-up): Welcome message. Acknowledge their goal specifically. Build excitement. Tell them exactly what to expect next. Keep it under 120 words. Message 2 (send 24 hours before first session): Prep message. What to bring/wear, what time to arrive, what the first session will look like. Remove all uncertainty. Include one motivational line at the end. Message 3 (send within 2 hours after first session): Post-session follow-up. Celebrate what they did. Mention one specific thing they did well. Preview what's coming next session. Ask how they're feeling. Under 100 words. Tone for all three: Warm, encouraging, professional. Like a coach who takes their results seriously.

Most training relationships are won or lost in the first two weeks. This sequence removes the awkward silence between sign-up and first session, sets professional expectations, and gives clients a reason to feel confident about their decision before they've even broken a sweat.

Template 3: The Re-Engagement Email for Lapsed Clients

Use this when: A former client has been quiet for 60–90 days. They haven't officially cancelled — they just faded. A well-timed, personal re-engagement message gets a meaningful percentage of them back. A generic "we miss you!" blast gets ignored.

Write a re-engagement email to a former personal training client who stopped coming in 2-3 months ago. The goal is to restart the conversation — not to hard-sell them. Client name: [First name] Their original goal: [e.g., lost 30 lbs before a wedding / was training for a 5K / post-baby fitness comeback] Approximate progress they made before stopping: [e.g., lost 12 lbs, ran their first 5K, got back to pre-baby weight] Why they might have stopped (if I know): [e.g., life got busy, moved, had a baby, work travel — or leave blank if unknown] Any new offer or change I can mention: [e.g., new morning slots available / added online coaching / new group format / nothing specific] The email should: - Reference their specific progress so they know it's not a mass email - Acknowledge that life happens — zero guilt - Plant a seed about what's possible in the next 90 days if they restart now - Make it easy to say yes (specific next step: "reply to this email" or "book a free check-in call") - Close warmly without being desperate Length: 150-200 words. Subject line included. No pushy sales language.
💡 When to send this: Set a calendar reminder 60 days after a client's last session. If you haven't heard from them, send the re-engagement email that day. The window is short — after 90 days, the emotional connection fades fast and they've usually found something else.

Template 4: Workout Program Description (for selling online programs)

Use this when: You're launching or listing an online training program — on your website, Gumroad, a coaching platform, or social media. Most trainers write program descriptions that lead with features ("12 weeks, 4 days per week, progressive overload"). The ones that sell lead with outcomes and speak directly to the buyer's situation. This prompt flips the order.

Write a sales-oriented workout program description for a [online / PDF / app-based] fitness program I'm selling. Program name: [What you're calling it] Program length: [e.g., 8 weeks, 12 weeks] Training days per week: [e.g., 3 days per week] Equipment required: [e.g., dumbbells only / full gym / bodyweight only / resistance bands] Target audience: [be specific — e.g., women over 40 who want to lose weight without cardio / beginner men who've never lifted / busy professionals with 30 min/day] Main outcome the buyer will achieve: [e.g., lose 10-15 lbs / build visible muscle definition / run their first 5K / do their first pull-up] Price: $[amount] Write: 1. A headline (under 12 words) that leads with the outcome, not the format 2. A 3-sentence opening paragraph that speaks directly to the buyer's frustration before they found this program 3. A "What you get" section with 5-6 bullets (mix of content features AND emotional benefits) 4. A closing paragraph that reinforces the transformation and includes a clear call to action Tone: Direct, confident, empathetic. Not hype-y. Not clinical. Like a coach who's seen the results firsthand.

Template 5: The Testimonial Request (Sent After a Win)

Use this when: A client just hit a milestone — lost 20 pounds, finished their first race, hit a PR, hit 6 months of consistency. This is your window. Strike when the emotion is high. A well-crafted testimonial request gets a real response; a generic "can you leave a review?" gets ignored or produces something useless like "great trainer, highly recommend."

Write a testimonial request message to send to a personal training client who just hit a major milestone. Client name: [First name] Their milestone: [e.g., lost 22 lbs over 4 months / completed their first half-marathon / deadlifted 200 lbs for the first time / 6 months of consistent training] How long we've worked together: [e.g., 4 months / 8 months / 2 years] Where I want the testimonial to appear: [e.g., Google review / my website / social media / just a private written note I can use in marketing] The message should: - Celebrate their win first — make them feel how big this actually is - Make the ask feel natural, not transactional ("if you're willing" — not "please leave me a review") - Give them a specific structure so they don't freeze: [e.g., "What you were struggling with before → what changed → what you'd tell someone else thinking about coaching"] - Include a direct link placeholder if it's for Google or another review platform: [LINK] - End on a forward-looking note — not just the win, but where they're headed next Length: Under 150 words. Warm and personal.

Testimonials from real clients with real results are the most valuable marketing asset you have — more than any ad you'll ever run. One compelling story from a client who lost 30 pounds, came back from an injury, or ran their first marathon closes more new clients than a month of generic social media content. Don't leave them on the table.

How to Build This Into Your Weekly Workflow

The trainers who get the most out of these prompts aren't sitting down every time they need to write something. They build it into a system. Here's what works:

Once a month: Batch all your client re-engagement emails. Pull anyone who's been quiet for 60+ days, run the prompt for each, and schedule them to send over the next two weeks.

Weekly (check-in day): Update your client notes doc with last session details. Run all your check-in prompts in one ChatGPT session. Done in 10 minutes instead of 45.

After every new sign-up: Run the onboarding sequence the same day. Set it up to send at the right intervals. New clients feel immediately taken care of — which is exactly the first impression you want to make.

After every client milestone: Send the testimonial request within 24 hours of the win. Emotion drives action — the longer you wait, the more diluted the response.
