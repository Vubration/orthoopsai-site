Why One-Line Prompts Fail

Most business owners get frustrated with AI because they try prompts like: "Write me a follow-up email."

What comes back is vague, corporate, and totally unusable. They figure AI just isn't ready yet. They're wrong — the prompt isn't ready yet.

A working prompt has four parts:

  • Role — Tell the AI who it is ("You are a contractor writing to a residential client…")
  • Task — Be specific about what you need ("Write a 3-email follow-up sequence for an unanswered estimate")
  • Constraints — Set the guardrails ("Keep it under 150 words. Professional but warm. No pushy language.")
  • Variables — Leave blanks for your specifics ("[CLIENT NAME]", "[ESTIMATE AMOUNT]", "[SERVICE TYPE]")

When you build prompts that way, you stop fighting the AI and start multiplying yourself.

Prompt #1 — The Universal Client Proposal

Every service business needs this. Whether you're a lawyer quoting a case, a contractor estimating a renovation, or a fitness coach pitching a 12-week program — this prompt builds a clean, professional proposal that covers scope, price, and next steps.

Copy this prompt → You are a professional [TYPE OF BUSINESS] writing a client proposal. Your tone is confident, clear, and results-focused — not salesy. Write a proposal that includes: (1) a brief acknowledgment of the client's specific situation, (2) what you will deliver and what the deliverable looks like, (3) your fee or pricing structure, (4) a clear timeline from start to finish, and (5) a single specific next step for the client to take. My business: [YOUR BUSINESS NAME AND WHAT YOU DO] Client situation: [2-3 SENTENCES DESCRIBING WHAT THEY NEED] What I'm offering: [SERVICE NAME AND BRIEF DESCRIPTION] Price: [AMOUNT AND PAYMENT STRUCTURE] Timeline: [START DATE AND COMPLETION DATE OR DURATION] Next step: [WHAT YOU WANT THEM TO DO — SIGN, CALL, DEPOSIT, ETC.]

Use this any time you're following up on a consultation, quoting a job, or converting a warm lead. It closes faster than a casual "let me know what you think" email.

Prompt #2 — The 3-Touch Follow-Up Sequence

Seventy percent of sales happen after the fifth contact — but most business owners give up after one. This prompt builds a complete 3-email follow-up sequence for any unanswered pitch, proposal, or consultation.

Copy this prompt → You are a [TYPE OF BUSINESS PROFESSIONAL] writing a 3-email follow-up sequence for a prospect who has not responded to an initial outreach or proposal. The sequence should feel human and not desperate. Email 1 (Day 3): Brief, friendly check-in — assume they're busy, not uninterested. Email 2 (Day 7): Add a small piece of value — a tip, a fact, or a relevant example — then gently revisit the offer. Email 3 (Day 14): A soft closing message — leave the door open without pressure. My business: [YOUR BUSINESS NAME] What I offered them: [SERVICE OR PRODUCT] Their situation: [WHAT THEY CAME TO YOU FOR — ONE SENTENCE] Tone: [PROFESSIONAL / WARM AND FRIENDLY / DIRECT]

This alone is worth more than most "sales training" programs. Consistent follow-up is the single highest-ROI activity in any service business — and now you can automate it in under 5 minutes.

Prompt #3 — Google Review Request (After a Win)

You know you need more Google reviews. You also know that asking feels awkward. This prompt writes a review request message that feels natural — delivered right after you've completed a job well done, when the client is at peak satisfaction.

Copy this prompt → You are a [TYPE OF BUSINESS] writing a short, personal text or email to a client right after completing their job. The goal is to ask for a Google review in a way that feels warm and personal — not like a mass message. Reference something specific about their project. Keep it under 100 words. End with the Google review link as a natural CTA. My business: [YOUR BUSINESS NAME] Client name: [FIRST NAME] What we did for them: [ONE SENTENCE DESCRIBING THE JOB OR SERVICE] What they seemed most happy about: [ONE SPECIFIC THING — "the quick turnaround," "how clean we left the site," etc.] My Google review link: [YOUR LINK]

One extra review per week compounds into a massive competitive advantage within 12 months. This prompt makes it happen consistently.

Prompt #4 — The FAQ Generator (For Any Service)

FAQs on your website reduce friction, answer objections before they come up, and help you rank in search. But writing them from scratch is tedious. This prompt generates 8 realistic, useful FAQs based on your service in a single shot.

Copy this prompt → You are a content strategist writing a FAQ section for a [TYPE OF BUSINESS] website. Generate 8 frequently asked questions that real potential clients would actually have before hiring this type of business. For each question, write a clear, direct, 2-4 sentence answer that builds trust and removes objections. Do not use filler phrases. Vary the question styles — include process questions, pricing questions, timeline questions, and outcome questions. My business: [BUSINESS NAME AND BRIEF DESCRIPTION] Target client: [WHO TYPICALLY HIRES YOU — e.g., "homeowners needing bathroom renovations" or "small business owners needing bookkeeping"] Main services: [LIST 3-5 CORE SERVICES] What makes you different: [1-2 SENTENCES ABOUT YOUR EDGE]

Drop these directly onto your website's FAQ page or service pages. They'll answer the most common sales objections before a prospect ever picks up the phone.

Prompt #5 — The "Dead Lead" Re-Engagement Email

You have people in your inbox or CRM who asked about your services 3-6 months ago, then went quiet. They're not gone — they might just be waiting for the right moment. This prompt wakes them up.

Copy this prompt → You are a [TYPE OF BUSINESS PROFESSIONAL] writing a re-engagement email to a prospect who reached out several months ago but never moved forward. The tone must be low-pressure and genuine. DO NOT mention that they "disappeared" or "went quiet" — simply acknowledge time has passed and you're checking in. Include one line about a relevant change or new offer that might shift the calculus for them. End with a single, easy call to action (reply, book a call, or request a quote). My business: [BUSINESS NAME] Their original interest: [WHAT THEY ASKED ABOUT] Time since last contact: [TIMEFRAME — "about 4 months ago"] New development to mention: [SOMETHING RECENT — new pricing, new availability, seasonal offer, new service] Call to action: [ONE SPECIFIC EASY THING TO DO]

Every business has a graveyard of "almost clients." A well-timed re-engagement email can convert 10-20% of them. That's money you've already spent acquiring that lead — you might as well collect it.
