Why Your Staff Doesn't Upsell (And Why That's Your Fault)

Staff don't upsell for three reasons, and none of them are laziness:

  1. They don't know what to say. "You want fries with that?" works because it's scripted. Without a script, staff default to silence — it feels less awkward than a clumsy pitch.
  2. They're afraid of seeming pushy. Most employees would rather lose a sale than make a customer feel pressured. That instinct is actually correct. The problem is they don't have a version of asking that doesn't feel pushy.
  3. Inconsistency kills confidence. If upselling happens only when someone remembers to ask, it never becomes natural. Natural only comes from repetition, and repetition only comes from a script said every single time.

The fix: write them a script so good it doesn't feel like a script. That's what the AI prompt below does.

💡 The difference between upselling and suggesting is framing. "Would you like to add that?" is a sale. "Those just came out of the oven — they're really good with an Americano" is insider information. One creates pressure. The other creates an experience. Your staff can deliver the second version without feeling like they're selling.

The AI Prompt That Writes It For You

Here's Prompt 42 from the Small Business Owner AI Toolkit — paste this into ChatGPT or Claude, fill in the brackets, and you'll have a ready-to-use upsell script in 30 seconds:

Write a natural, non-pushy in-person upsell script for staff at [BUSINESS NAME] to use at checkout. The customer is buying [MAIN PRODUCT OR SERVICE]. The upsell is: [DESCRIBE ADD-ON OR UPGRADE]. The script should feel like a helpful suggestion, not a hard sell. Write it as exact spoken words — 2–3 sentences max. Train staff to say this every time.

The result sounds like a recommendation from a friend who works there. Example for a coffee shop:

"Those lemon bars just came out of the oven about an hour ago — they pair really well with an Americano if you want to grab one while they're still warm."

That's not a sale. That's insider information. Customers appreciate it — and they buy.

Real Examples Across Business Types

Here's what the prompt generates for different business types. Notice how none of them sound like an upsell:

Hair salon (upselling a conditioning treatment):
"With the color we're doing today, your ends are going to be a little more porous than usual. I'd actually recommend the 10-minute treatment after your rinse — it seals everything in and your color will last 2–3 weeks longer. It's only $15 and I really think you'll notice the difference."

Auto shop (upselling a tire rotation):
"We noticed your tires are getting pretty uneven wear on the fronts — we can rotate them while you're here today. It'll run you $25 and it's the kind of thing that extends tire life by 15,000 miles, so it's usually worth doing when we already have the car in the air."

Restaurant (upselling a dessert):
"Just so you know, the chocolate lava cake takes 12 minutes to prep so if you think you want dessert, I'd put that order in now. We make it fresh and it's the one thing people always say they wish they'd ordered."

Gym (upselling a personal training session):
"Since you're working on your deadlift, it might be worth doing a single session with Marcus — he does a free 20-minute form check for members and a lot of people end up fixing problems they didn't even know they had. Want me to see if he has time Thursday?"

💡 Notice the pattern: every script leads with a reason, not an offer. "Your ends will be porous." "Your tires have uneven wear." "The lava cake takes 12 minutes." The reason comes first, which makes the offer feel like a logical conclusion rather than a transaction.

Why "Every Time" Is the Most Important Part

The prompt instruction says "train staff to say this every time." That phrase is doing more work than it looks like.

If staff only asks when they feel like it, it becomes inconsistent and awkward. The customer has no context — some visits they hear it, some they don't. That inconsistency is what makes upselling feel random and pushy.

When it's said every time:

  • It becomes part of the experience, not an interruption to it
  • Customers don't feel singled out — it's just what happens here
  • Staff gets comfortable quickly because they're not improvising
  • You can actually track whether it's working

Think about how a movie theater employee says "Would you like the medium for only 50 cents more?" at every single transaction. Nobody feels pressured by it. It's just part of the process. That's the goal.

How to Roll This Out With Your Team

Getting staff to actually use the script takes one step most owners skip: practice. Reading a script feels different from saying it out loud in context. Here's a simple rollout:

  1. Generate the script with the AI prompt. Customize the brackets for your business and your top upsell item.
  2. Read it out loud twice. It should take under 10 seconds. If it doesn't, it's too long.
  3. Run one practice session at your next team meeting. Two minutes: one person plays customer, another delivers the script. No stakes, just repetition.
  4. Post it at the register. For the first two weeks, staff can glance at it. It should be memorized within a week of regular use.
  5. Track it for 30 days. Count how many times it was said (use a tally sheet), how many times it landed, and how much additional revenue moved.
💡 Generate one script per upsell item, not one universal script. The reason comes first — and the reason is specific to what's being bought. A generic "would you like to add anything?" is useless. A specific "since you're getting the basic facial, the LED light therapy pairs really well with it and it's 20% off today" converts.
